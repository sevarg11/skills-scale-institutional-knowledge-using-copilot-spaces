OctoAcme runs projects through a clear lifecycle—initiation, planning, execution, release, and close—grounded in principles like iterative delivery, clear ownership, and data-informed decisions. Early work centers on a lightweight Project One-pager to define problem, goals, and success metrics; planning converts that into a prioritized backlog, Definition of Done, and release milestones; execution uses project boards and small, testable increments; and closing includes retrospectives to capture improvements.

Core workflows emphasize a visual project board (Backlog, Ready, In Progress, In Review, QA, Done), a disciplined PR process (small PRs when possible, link issues and acceptance criteria, CI tests and linting, and at least one approval before merge), and sprint planning that pulls ready items meeting the DoD. Planning activities include kickoff, backlog prioritization and estimation, dependency mapping, and a release plan with milestone mapping. The docs also call out practical artifacts—roadmaps, risk registers, acceptance criteria templates, and release notes—to keep work traceable and auditable.

Roles and responsibilities are explicitly defined: Product Managers (PdM) own what gets built and success metrics; Project Managers (PM) coordinate delivery, schedules, risks, and communications; Developers implement and test features; QA validates acceptance criteria and test plans; and stakeholders provide inputs and approvals. These personas guide who does what across initiation, planning, execution, and retrospectives, and the docs recommend naming PM and Product Lead for each project to ensure clear ownership.

Communication and quality assurance are tightly integrated into the process. The team rhythm includes daily standups for immediate triage, weekly delivery syncs, regular demos at sprint/milestone end, and monthly stakeholder updates. Risk and dependency tracking live in a Risk Register with defined escalation paths (team → PM → Product Lead → Sponsor) and communication templates for status and incidents. QA practices require unit and integration tests, end-to-end smoke tests for critical flows, CI security scanning, and manual QA where needed; releases follow a pre-release checklist and a rollback/incident playbook to minimize production risk.
